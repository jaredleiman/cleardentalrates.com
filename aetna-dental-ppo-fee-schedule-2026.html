Aetna Dental PPO Fee Schedule 2026: What Changed and What Practices Need to Do

May 20267 min readClearDentalRates.com

Aetna Dental introduced one of the more complicated 2026 fee schedule updates among major carriers — modest reductions on high-volume restorative codes paired with selective increases on periodontal procedures. The net impact for a typical general practice depends heavily on procedure mix, but most Aetna-participating offices are seeing flat-to-slightly-negative aggregate changes year over year.

What Changed in Aetna's 2026 Fee Schedule

Aetna's PPO fee schedule update took effect January 1, 2026, with the most significant changes concentrated in restorative dentistry. D2740 (porcelain crown) and D2391 (one-surface composite) saw modest reductions, while several periodontal codes — most notably D4341 and D4342 — received small upward adjustments. The pattern suggests Aetna is rebalancing its schedule to reflect higher utilization in periodontal services.

May 2026 Intelligence Alert

Aetna Dental PPO reduced D2740 reimbursement by approximately 2.4% and D2391 by 1.8% effective January 1, 2026. For a practice billing 8 crowns weekly to Aetna patients, the D2740 reduction alone represents roughly $9,800 in annual revenue impact.

Aetna's 2026 changes follow a pattern the carrier began in 2024, when it started decoupling restorative and preventive fee changes from broader network averages. Where Humana and Delta have applied broader percentage cuts to preventive codes, Aetna has focused its 2026 reductions on higher-dollar restorative procedures — meaning per-claim revenue impact is larger but claim volume is lower.

Why Aetna Targeted Restorative Codes This Year

Aetna's restorative-focused reductions reflect a structural shift in how the carrier views procedure-level cost control. Restorative codes — particularly crowns — have higher per-claim dollar values, so even small percentage reductions translate to meaningful savings at network scale. From the practice side, the math is the inverse: each reduced D2740 represents real dollars per case, and the impact compounds quickly for restoration-heavy practices.

The selective increases on D4341 and D4342 are equally notable. Periodontal scaling utilization has risen across most carrier networks since 2023, and Aetna's modest rate increase appears to be a network-stability move rather than a generosity gesture — keeping participating providers from disengaging in a procedure category where utilization continues climbing.

Aetna's Renegotiation Window

Aetna's fee schedule renegotiation window for in-network providers typically opens in late summer, with most rate change requests needing to be submitted between August and October for the following calendar year. This is a tighter window than Humana or Delta, and Aetna's provider relations team has historically required more extensive documentation for fee review requests.

For 2027 rates, practices that want to contest the D2740 or D2391 reductions should plan to initiate the conversation with their Aetna provider relations representative no later than August 2026. Document your annual Aetna patient volume, the specific codes affected, and your UCR fees for those codes before reaching out.

How to Respond to Aetna's 2026 Changes

Option 1: Run the Restorative Volume Math

Pull your last 12 months of Aetna claims and isolate D2740, D2391, D2392, and D2950 utilization. The aggregate dollar impact of the 2026 reductions across these four codes is the number that matters — not the individual percentage changes. Practices with high restorative volume on Aetna patients are absorbing the largest hit.

Option 2: Request a Targeted Fee Review

Aetna provider relations will rarely revisit an entire fee schedule mid-year, but the carrier has historically been willing to review specific codes when a participating practice provides documented utilization data. Lead with the codes where you have the highest volume — that's where Aetna has the most network-stability incentive to negotiate.

Option 3: Evaluate Aetna's Network Tier Structure

Aetna operates multiple PPO network tiers, and not all are subject to the same fee schedule. Some practices participating in Aetna's broader networks may find they're being paid the lower-tier schedule even though their contract should specify a higher-tier rate. This is worth verifying directly in the provider portal — it's a common, fixable error that can be worth thousands annually.
